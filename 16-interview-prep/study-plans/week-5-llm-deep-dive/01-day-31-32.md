# Day 31-32: Architecture, Scaling & Efficient Training/Inference

## Why This Topic Comes Here

Weeks 1-4 build general ML fluency; LLM interviews expect that plus mechanism-level depth on transformer internals and the specific engineering tricks that make training and serving them tractable at scale. These two days build that depth before Week 5 moves on to alignment and applied systems.

---

## Day 31: Architecture & Scaling

### Transformer Internals

- **Self-attention**: $\text{softmax}(QK^T/\sqrt{d_k})V$. The $\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling prevents dot products from growing large in magnitude as dimensionality increases, which would push softmax into saturated (near-zero-gradient) regions.
- **Multi-head attention**: splits $Q,K,V$ into $h$ heads so the model can attend to different representation subspaces in parallel, then concatenates and projects.
- **Position encoding**: sinusoidal (fixed, extrapolates poorly), learned (fixed max length), RoPE (rotary — relative position baked into the dot product, dominant in modern LLMs), ALiBi (linear bias, extrapolates well to longer sequences).

### Scaling Laws

- Chinchilla scaling: for a fixed compute budget, model size and training tokens should scale roughly equally — most pre-Chinchilla models (GPT-3) were undertrained relative to their parameter count.
- Loss scales as a power law in compute, data, and parameters — diminishing returns, not a hard ceiling, but the exponent tells you where to invest additional compute.
- **Key insight:** "Bigger model" and "more data" are not interchangeable levers — Chinchilla's contribution was showing the optimal ratio between them for a given compute budget.

### Training Stability

- Loss spikes mid-training are common at scale; causes include bad data batches, learning rate too high relative to batch size, or numerical instability in low-precision (bf16/fp16) accumulation.
- Mitigations: gradient clipping, warmup + cosine decay LR schedules, careful init (e.g., scaled residual init), and checkpoint-rollback-and-skip on spike detection.

### MoE (Mixture of Experts) Routing

- Replaces a single dense FFN with $N$ expert FFNs; a router selects the top-$k$ experts per token, so inference compute stays sparse (roughly constant) even as total parameter count grows.
- **Load balancing problem**: without an auxiliary loss, the router collapses to favoring a few experts — starving the rest of training signal. Fixed with a load-balancing auxiliary loss that penalizes uneven expert utilization.
- Tradeoff: MoE increases total parameters (and memory footprint) without a proportional increase in FLOPs per token — good for compute-constrained, memory-rich serving setups.

## Day 32: Efficient Training & Inference

### Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning

- **LoRA**: freezes the pretrained weight matrix $W$ and learns a low-rank decomposition $\Delta W = BA$ (rank $r \ll d$), added at inference time. Reduces trainable parameters by orders of magnitude while matching full fine-tuning quality on most tasks.
- **QLoRA**: LoRA on top of a 4-bit quantized base model — enables fine-tuning models far larger than would otherwise fit in GPU memory, at a small quality cost from quantization.
- **Key insight:** LoRA works because the weight *update* needed for task adaptation empirically has low intrinsic rank, even though the full weight matrix does not.

### KV Cache, MQA, GQA

- **KV cache**: caches Key/Value projections for all previously generated tokens so autoregressive decoding avoids recomputing them each step — turns per-step cost from growing with full sequence attention into a single new-token computation.
- **Multi-Query Attention (MQA)**: all heads share a single K/V projection, cutting KV cache memory by a factor of (num heads), at some quality cost.
- **Grouped-Query Attention (GQA)**: a middle ground — groups of heads share K/V projections, trading off memory savings against quality closer to full multi-head attention. Used in most modern production LLMs.

### Quantization

- Reducing weight precision fp32 → fp16 → int8 → int4; each step roughly halves memory and can meaningfully speed up memory-bound inference.
- Post-training quantization is cheap but can degrade quality unpredictably on outlier-heavy layers; quantization-aware training or techniques like GPTQ/AWQ mitigate this.

### Speculative Decoding

- A small, fast draft model proposes several tokens; the large target model verifies them in a single forward pass, accepting matches and resampling from the first divergence.
- Gives 2-4x wall-clock speedup with **no change to the output distribution** — this guarantee (verified via rejection sampling) is what distinguishes it from just using a smaller model.
