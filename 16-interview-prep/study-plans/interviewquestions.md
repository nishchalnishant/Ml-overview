# Consolidated Interview Question Bank (4-Week Study Plan)

This file consolidates interview-style questions and answers spanning the entire 30-day study plan, now reorganized by difficulty tier (Easy / Medium / Hard) so you can drill progressively. Use it as a rapid-recall drill: cover the answer, read the question, and check whether your explanation includes the mechanism (not just the label).

---

## Easy

#### Q: What is the bias-variance tradeoff, and how does it show up in model selection?
Bias is the error from overly simplistic assumptions (the model can't capture the true pattern); variance is the error from sensitivity to the specific training sample (the model captures noise as if it were signal). Total expected error decomposes as $\text{Error} = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance} + \text{Irreducible Noise}$. A linear model on non-linear data has high bias, low variance. A deep, unpruned decision tree has low bias, high variance. Regularization, pruning, and ensembling (bagging) are all variance-reduction tools; adding model capacity or features is a bias-reduction tool. Example: increasing polynomial degree in regression reduces bias but increases variance — cross-validated error follows a U-shape as you plot it against model complexity, and the goal is to sit at the minimum of that curve, not to zero-out either term in isolation.

#### Q: Why is Occam's Razor relevant to ML, and does it always mean "pick the simplest model"?
Occam's Razor argues that among models with similar fit to the data, the simpler one generalizes better because it is less likely to have fit noise. In ML terms, this underlies regularization (L1/L2 penalties) and the preference for interpretable baselines before complex architectures. It does not mean "always use the simplest possible model" — it means simplicity should be preferred *only when predictive performance is comparable*. A gotcha: "simple" should be measured by effective capacity (e.g., number of free parameters relative to data size), not by algorithm name — a shallow decision tree with unlimited depth per split can still overfit badly despite sounding "simple."

#### Q: State the No Free Lunch theorem in your own words. Why does it matter practically?
No Free Lunch says that averaged over all possible problems, no algorithm outperforms any other — an algorithm's success is always tied to how well its inductive biases match the structure of the specific problem. Practically, this means you cannot claim "XGBoost is the best algorithm" in the abstract; you can only claim it performs well on tabular data with mixed feature types and non-linear interactions, which is a common but not universal problem structure. Interview gotcha: this theorem is often used to justify "always try multiple algorithms and let validation performance decide" rather than defaulting to a favorite model.

#### Q: Explain the difference between standardization and min-max normalization. When would you use each?
Standardization (z-score: $\frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$) centers data at mean 0 with unit variance and does not bound the range; it's preferred for algorithms assuming roughly Gaussian-distributed features or that rely on distances/gradients where the same scale across features matters (SVM, logistic regression, neural networks, PCA). Min-max normalization ($\frac{x - \min}{\max - \min}$) rescales to a fixed range like [0,1]; it's useful when you need bounded inputs (e.g., image pixel values, some neural net activation ranges) but is sensitive to outliers since a single extreme value compresses the rest of the distribution. Gotcha: tree-based models (Decision Trees, Random Forest, XGBoost) are invariant to monotonic feature scaling because they split on thresholds, not distances — scaling them is unnecessary, though harmless.

#### Q: What's the difference between a parameter and a hyperparameter? Give examples of each.
Parameters are learned automatically from data during training via the optimization process (e.g., the weights and biases of a neural network, the coefficients of a linear regression, the split thresholds of a decision tree). Hyperparameters are set before training begins and control the learning process or model structure itself — they are not learned from the training data via gradient descent/loss minimization (e.g., learning rate, number of trees, `max_depth`, regularization strength $\lambda$, number of clusters $k$). Hyperparameters are typically chosen via a separate search process (grid/random/Bayesian search) evaluated on a validation set, precisely because you cannot fit them the same way you fit parameters — there's no gradient of validation loss with respect to "number of trees" in the same sense.

#### Q: What is TF-IDF, and why is it usually better than raw word counts?
TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency) is $TF(t,d) \times IDF(t) = \frac{f_{t,d}}{|d|} \times \log\frac{N}{df_t}$, where TF measures how often a term appears within a document (normalized by document length) and IDF down-weights terms that appear in many documents across the corpus (like "the", "is") since they carry little discriminative information. Raw counts alone over-weight both very long documents (more words = higher counts) and extremely common words that dominate frequency but say nothing distinctive about a document's topic. Gotcha: TF-IDF still treats words as context-independent tokens — a rare word could get a high IDF weight purely because it's a rare misspelling or a noisy artifact in the corpus (e.g., an OCR error), not because it's semantically meaningful; inspecting the top TF-IDF terms per document is a useful sanity check.

#### Q: Explain Gini impurity vs. entropy for decision tree splits — does the choice matter?
Gini impurity: $G = 1 - \sum_k p_k^2$, range [0, 0.5] for binary classification. Entropy: $H = -\sum_k p_k \log_2 p_k$, range [0,1]. Both measure node "impurity" and the tree greedily picks the split that maximizes impurity reduction (information gain). In practice they produce nearly identical trees; entropy penalizes highly impure (50/50) nodes slightly more due to the log term, and Gini is marginally cheaper to compute (no log). Example calculation: a node with 80 positive, 20 negative has $p_+=0.8$, Gini $= 1 - (0.8^2+0.2^2) = 1-0.68=0.32$; entropy $=-(0.8\log_2 0.8+0.2\log_2 0.2)\approx0.72$. The more consequential hyperparameters are `max_depth`, `min_samples_leaf`, and `min_impurity_decrease` — pruning controls, not the impurity metric choice.

#### Q: What is the kernel trick in SVMs, and why is it computationally efficient?
The kernel trick lets an SVM operate as if it mapped data into a much higher-dimensional (even infinite-dimensional) feature space where classes become linearly separable, without ever explicitly computing the coordinates in that space. This works because the SVM optimization and prediction only ever require dot products between pairs of examples, and a kernel function $K(x, x')$ computes what that dot product *would be* in the higher-dimensional space directly from the original-space vectors. The RBF kernel $K(x,x') = \exp(-\gamma\|x-x'\|^2)$ implicitly corresponds to an infinite-dimensional feature map. Gotcha: the kernel trick doesn't "transform the data" — no new representation is stored; it's purely a computational shortcut for computing similarity.

#### Q: What is a p-value, in plain language, and what is a common misinterpretation?
A p-value is the probability of observing data at least as extreme as what you actually observed, *assuming the null hypothesis is true*. It is NOT the probability that the null hypothesis is true, and it is NOT the probability that your result happened by chance — this is the most common misinterpretation. A p-value of 0.03 means "if there really were no effect, we'd see data this extreme or more only 3% of the time" — it says nothing directly about the probability that there is no effect. Related concept: Type I error (false positive — rejecting a true null) is controlled by the significance threshold α; Type II error (false negative — failing to reject a false null) is controlled by statistical power, which depends on sample size and effect size.

#### Q: What's the difference between correlation measured by Pearson vs. Spearman, and when would they disagree?
Pearson correlation measures the strength of a *linear* relationship and is sensitive to outliers since it's computed from raw values. Spearman correlation measures the strength of a *monotonic* relationship by correlating the ranks of the values, making it robust to outliers and able to detect non-linear-but-monotonic relationships (e.g., $y = x^3$) that Pearson would underestimate. They disagree most visibly when the relationship is monotonic but curved (Spearman near 1, Pearson lower) or when a few extreme outliers dominate the Pearson calculation while barely affecting the rank-based Spearman. Gotcha: neither captures non-monotonic relationships (e.g., a U-shape) — both can report near-zero correlation for a strong quadratic relationship, which is why EDA should always include scatter plots, not just correlation coefficients.

#### Q: Why should dropout be disabled during inference, and what happens if you leave it on?
During training, dropout randomly zeroes a fraction $p$ of neurons on each forward pass, forcing the network to not rely on any single neuron and approximating an ensemble average over $2^N$ thinned sub-networks. During inference, you want the full, deterministic network's prediction — but since the weights were trained with a fraction of neurons zeroed on average, the expected magnitude of a layer's output changes if you suddenly use all neurons without adjustment. Standard implementations solve this via "inverted dropout": during training, activations are additionally scaled up by $1/(1-p)$ so that at inference, no scaling is needed and dropout is simply turned off. If you leave dropout on during inference, predictions become stochastic (different each call) and the effective output distribution shifts from what the model was calibrated for, typically degrading and destabilizing performance — this is also why forgetting `model.eval()` in PyTorch is a classic bug.

#### Q: Why does F1 score use the harmonic mean of precision and recall instead of the arithmetic mean?
The harmonic mean penalizes extreme imbalance between precision and recall much more heavily than the arithmetic mean does. Example: precision = 1.0, recall = 0.01. Arithmetic mean = 0.505, which looks like a mediocre-but-usable model. Harmonic mean (F1) $= \frac{2 \times 1.0 \times 0.01}{1.0+0.01} \approx 0.0198$, correctly reflecting that a model catching only 1% of positives is nearly useless regardless of its precision. The harmonic mean is dominated by the smaller of the two values, which is exactly the behavior you want when both precision and recall must be reasonably good for the model to be useful.

#### Q: How does K-Means work, and what are its core limitations?
K-Means iteratively (1) assigns each point to its nearest of $k$ centroids, (2) recomputes each centroid as the mean of its assigned points, and repeats until assignments stabilize, minimizing within-cluster sum of squares (inertia). Limitations: it assumes spherical, similarly-sized clusters (fails on elongated or nested clusters), requires you to pre-specify $k$ (use the elbow method on inertia vs. k, or silhouette score, to choose), is sensitive to centroid initialization (use k-means++ to mitigate — random initialization can converge to poor local optima), and is sensitive to feature scale (always standardize features first since it uses Euclidean distance) and to outliers (a single far-away point can drag a centroid).

#### Q: Why do we need non-linear activation functions in neural networks?
Without a non-linear activation, stacking any number of linear layers is mathematically equivalent to a single linear layer (the composition of linear functions is linear), so a "deep" network with only linear activations has the same representational capacity as a single-layer linear model — no matter how many layers you add. Non-linear activations (ReLU, sigmoid, tanh, GELU) are what let the network approximate arbitrary non-linear decision boundaries and functions, which the Universal Approximation Theorem formalizes: a sufficiently wide single-hidden-layer network with non-linear activation can approximate any continuous function on a compact domain (though the theorem says nothing about how *easy* that function is to learn via gradient descent, or how wide "sufficiently wide" needs to be — depth is often more parameter-efficient in practice than width).

#### Q: What's the difference between PCA for feature extraction and t-SNE for visualization — can either be plugged directly into a downstream model?
PCA is feature *extraction*: it creates new features (principal components) as linear combinations of the originals, ordered by how much variance they capture, and it has a well-defined transform you can apply consistently to new/unseen data — so PCA output can be used as model input. t-SNE optimizes purely for a 2D/3D visual layout by preserving local neighborhood structure; it distorts global distances non-linearly, has no consistent out-of-sample transform, and its output coordinates carry no principled numeric meaning beyond visualization. Use PCA when you need reusable, deterministic dimensionality reduction for modeling; use t-SNE only to visually inspect cluster structure, never as an actual model input feature.

#### Q: Compare grid search and random search for hyperparameter tuning — which is generally preferred and why?
Grid search exhaustively evaluates every combination in a predefined grid — simple and reproducible, but suffers combinatorial explosion as the number of hyperparameters grows, and wastes budget evaluating unpromising regions uniformly. Random search samples hyperparameter combinations randomly (often from log-uniform distributions for parameters like learning rate) and is generally preferred for the same budget when only a few hyperparameters actually matter, because it doesn't waste evaluations on redundant combinations along unimportant dimensions — it explores more distinct values per important dimension than a grid of the same total size.

#### Q: Explain Bayes' theorem and walk through a base-rate neglect example.
Bayes' theorem: $P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) P(A)}{P(B)}$. Base-rate neglect is the common intuition failure of ignoring the prior probability. Example: a disease test is 99% accurate (both sensitivity and specificity) and the disease has 0.1% prevalence. Given a positive test, what's $P(\text{disease} | \text{positive})$? Using Bayes: $P(D|+) = \frac{0.99 \times 0.001}{0.99 \times 0.001 + 0.01 \times 0.999} = \frac{0.00099}{0.00099 + 0.00999} \approx 0.09$, so only about 9% — despite the test being "99% accurate." Most people intuitively guess ~99%. This matters directly in ML for imbalanced classification: a model's precision on a rare positive class will be low even with a strong classifier, purely because false positives from the (much larger) negative class outnumber true positives from the rare class.

#### Q: What is a KV cache in autoregressive LLM generation, and what basic problem does it solve?
During autoregressive generation, each new token's attention computation needs the Key and Value projections of all previously generated tokens. Recomputing these from scratch at every generation step would mean each step's cost grows with the sequence length already generated. The KV cache stores the Key and Value tensors for all previous tokens so each new step only computes K/V for the newest token and reuses the cached values for the rest, reducing the per-step incremental cost substantially and avoiding redundant recomputation across the whole generation.

#### Q: What is the difference between few-shot prompting and zero-shot prompting?
Zero-shot prompting asks the model to perform a task from an instruction alone, with no worked examples, relying entirely on knowledge learned during pretraining/fine-tuning to infer the intended format and behavior. Few-shot prompting includes several (typically 3-8) complete input/output examples directly in the prompt, letting the model use in-context learning to infer the desired task format and behavior from the pattern of examples. Few-shot generally improves reliability on tasks where exact output format matters (e.g., a specific JSON schema) since the examples remove ambiguity that a zero-shot instruction alone might leave.

## Medium

#### Q: Why would you choose log-loss (cross-entropy) over MSE for a classification problem?
MSE treats the difference between prediction and label symmetrically and produces a non-convex loss surface when combined with the sigmoid activation, making optimization harder and gradients weak when the prediction is very wrong (because the sigmoid saturates and dMSE/dz shrinks in exactly the region where the gradient should be largest). Log-loss (cross-entropy) is convex in the model's logits for logistic regression and produces gradients proportional to the prediction error itself ($\hat{y} - y$), so being confidently wrong produces a large gradient. Concretely: for cross-entropy with sigmoid output, $\partial L/\partial z = \hat{y} - y$, which is a clean, well-scaled gradient regardless of how saturated the sigmoid is on the raw MSE.

#### Q: Could you use linear regression for a classification problem? What breaks?
Yes mechanically, but multiple things break: (1) predictions are unbounded and can fall outside [0,1], making them uninterpretable as probabilities; (2) the squared-error loss is minimized by a decision boundary that is sensitive to outliers far from the boundary, even though those points are already correctly classified — this is a specific and non-intuitive failure mode, since a "very correct" point can pull the boundary in the wrong direction; (3) it assumes homoscedastic, normally distributed residuals, which do not hold for binary outcomes. Logistic regression solves this by modeling log-odds linearly and squashing through a sigmoid, keeping outputs bounded and producing a convex loss surface.

#### Q: Walk through your process for handling missing data in a messy tabular dataset.
First classify the missingness mechanism: MCAR (missing completely at random — safe to drop or simple-impute), MAR (missingness depends on other observed features — condition on those features, e.g. regression/MICE imputation), or MNAR (missingness depends on the unobserved value itself — e.g., high earners not reporting income; simple imputation introduces systematic bias). For MCAR/MAR, options range from mean/median/mode (fast baseline, distorts variance) to KNN imputation (uses feature similarity) to MICE/iterative imputation (models each feature with missing values as a function of the others, iterating to convergence — most accurate but expensive). For MNAR, add a missingness indicator flag as an extra feature so the model can use "was this missing" as signal, since no imputation can honestly recover the value. Gotcha: never fit imputers on the full dataset before splitting — always fit on train only and transform test, or you leak information about the test distribution into training.

#### Q: What is data leakage and where does it hide in a preprocessing pipeline?
Data leakage is when information from outside the training data (typically from validation/test, or from the future in time-series) influences model training, producing inflated offline metrics that collapse in production. It hides in: (1) fitting scalers/imputers/encoders on the full dataset instead of train-only before splitting; (2) target encoding categorical variables using the full dataset's target statistics, effectively leaking the label into the feature (fix: compute per-fold target means with K-fold, or use leave-one-out encoding); (3) doing feature selection using the full dataset before cross-validation splits; (4) time-series problems where a feature is computed using future information relative to the prediction point. Prevention pattern: always wrap preprocessing in a `Pipeline`/`ColumnTransformer` fit inside each cross-validation fold, never outside it.

#### Q: Why is gradient descent slow or unstable when features are unscaled?
When features are on very different scales, the loss surface becomes an elongated, elliptical bowl rather than a circular one. A single global learning rate then either overshoots along the steep (large-scale) dimension or crawls along the shallow (small-scale) dimension, forcing you to pick an artificially small learning rate to avoid divergence on the steep axis — which then makes convergence painfully slow on the shallow axis. Standardizing features makes the loss surface closer to spherical, so a single learning rate works efficiently across all dimensions. This affects linear/logistic regression and neural networks directly; tree-based models are unaffected since they don't use gradient-based threshold search on raw feature magnitudes.

#### Q: What is target encoding and why is it prone to leakage? How do you fix it?
Target encoding replaces a categorical value with the mean of the target variable for that category (e.g., replace "zip code" with the average house price for that zip code). Naively computed on the full training set, each row's encoded value includes that row's own target in the average it's encoded with — this leaks the label directly into the feature, especially damaging for categories with few samples (a category with 1 row gets an encoding that IS its own label). Fix: compute encodings using K-fold cross-fitting (encode fold k using target means computed only from the other K-1 folds) or leave-one-out encoding (exclude the current row's own target from its category's average), and always add smoothing toward the global mean for low-count categories.

#### Q: You have a heavily right-skewed feature (e.g., income). What would you do and why?
First check with a histogram and skewness statistic (skew > 1 is heavily right-skewed). Right-skewed data with a long tail is common when the underlying process is multiplicative or has a natural floor at zero (income, word frequency, city population — power-law-like). Apply a log transform ($\log(x+1)$ if zeros are present) to compress the tail and make the distribution closer to symmetric/Gaussian, which helps linear models and algorithms assuming normally distributed errors, and makes outliers less dominant in distance-based methods. Gotcha: tree-based models don't need this transform since they split on rank order, not magnitude — log-transforming before a Random Forest is usually unnecessary (though harmless) but essential before linear regression, k-NN, or PCA.

#### Q: When would you use a t-test vs. Mann-Whitney U vs. ANOVA vs. Chi-squared?
Use a **t-test** to compare means of two groups when the data is approximately normally distributed (or n is large enough for CLT to apply) and variances are comparable. Use **Mann-Whitney U** (non-parametric alternative) when data is not normally distributed, is ordinal, or has significant outliers — it compares rank distributions instead of means. Use **ANOVA** to compare means across 3+ groups simultaneously (avoids inflating Type I error from running multiple pairwise t-tests); its non-parametric analog is **Kruskal-Wallis**. Use **Chi-squared** to test independence between two categorical variables (e.g., does conversion rate differ by marketing channel?) via a contingency table. Gotcha: running many pairwise t-tests instead of one ANOVA is the multiple comparisons problem — each individual test at α=0.05 compounds the overall false-positive rate across the whole family of tests.

#### Q: What is the multiple comparisons problem and how do you correct for it?
When you run many hypothesis tests simultaneously (e.g., testing 20 features for correlation with the target), the probability that at least one test produces a false positive purely by chance grows with the number of tests — at α=0.05 per test, testing 20 independent hypotheses gives roughly a 64% chance of at least one spurious "significant" result. Corrections: Bonferroni (divide α by the number of tests — simple but conservative, increases Type II error), or the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure (controls the false discovery rate, less conservative, more common in large-scale testing like genomics or feature screening). Gotcha in ML: this is why "p-hacking" via extensive feature-by-feature significance testing without correction is a common source of spurious features that fail to replicate on new data.

#### Q: Why does k-NN fail in high-dimensional spaces?
As dimensionality grows, the ratio of the distance to the nearest neighbor versus the farthest neighbor approaches 1 — essentially, all points become approximately equidistant from any given query point. This "curse of dimensionality" happens because volume grows exponentially with dimension, so data becomes increasingly sparse and the notion of a meaningful "neighborhood" breaks down geometrically. Practically, with 100,000 samples and 500 raw features, k-NN would need dimensionality reduction (PCA) or feature selection first, and would need scaled features (since Euclidean distance is dominated by whichever feature has the largest raw magnitude). Rule of thumb: k-NN needs $n \gg d$ to be reliable.

#### Q: Why must you scale features before SVM but not before a decision tree?
SVM's decision boundary and margin are computed from distances (dot products, Euclidean norms) between points. If one feature has a range 1000x larger than another, it will dominate the distance calculation and effectively determine the margin unilaterally, ignoring the smaller-scale feature's real predictive signal. Decision trees split on threshold comparisons per feature independently (`feature > 5.3`), so multiplying a feature by 1000 just rescales the threshold — the resulting split and rank ordering of examples is unaffected, meaning trees are scale-invariant.

#### Q: Explain bagging vs. boosting — what type of error does each address, and give an example algorithm for each.
Bagging reduces **variance** by averaging parallel models trained on bootstrapped samples (Random Forest); boosting reduces **bias** by sequentially fitting each model to the current ensemble's residuals (AdaBoost, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost). Full explanation, why Random Forest decorrelates trees via feature subsampling, and the XGBoost-overfitting lever order: see [week-2-algorithms/01-day-8-9-supervised-learning-algorithms.md](week-2-algorithms/01-day-8-9-supervised-learning-algorithms.md).

#### Q: K-Means requires numeric features and Euclidean distance. How would you cluster purely categorical data?
Standard K-Means' mean-centroid update and Euclidean distance don't make sense for categorical variables (there's no "average" category). Use **K-Modes**, which replaces the centroid with the mode (most frequent category) of each cluster along each dimension, and replaces Euclidean distance with a simple mismatch count (Hamming-like dissimilarity). For mixed categorical + numeric data, use **K-Prototypes**, which combines K-Means-style numeric distance with K-Modes-style categorical dissimilarity, weighted by a tunable parameter. Alternative: one-hot encode categoricals and run K-Means, though this can distort distances if there are many high-cardinality categorical columns (each category becomes a sparse dimension, diluting numeric feature contributions).

#### Q: What's the difference between using PCA for feature extraction vs. feature selection, and what does "explained variance" actually tell you?
PCA is feature *extraction*, not selection — it creates new features (principal components) as linear combinations of all original features, ordered by how much variance in the data they capture, rather than choosing a subset of existing, interpretable features. The explained variance ratio of a component tells you what fraction of the total variance in the data that component captures — but variance is not the same as predictive power for a downstream task. A component that captures 80% of the variance in the data might be irrelevant to the target label, while a low-variance component might be exactly what a classifier needs to distinguish classes. Gotcha: applying PCA before a supervised task blindly (without checking downstream task performance) can discard exactly the discriminative signal that has low variance in the input space (e.g., a rare but highly diagnostic feature).

#### Q: Explain backpropagation intuitively — what problem does it solve, and how?
Backpropagation efficiently computes the gradient of the loss with respect to every weight in a neural network by applying the chain rule from calculus, propagating the error signal backward from the output layer to the input layer, layer by layer. Without it, computing gradients for a deep network naively would require redundant recomputation of shared sub-terms; backprop reuses intermediate gradient computations (via dynamic-programming-like caching) so the total cost is roughly proportional to one forward pass, not exponential in depth. Concretely, for a chain $z = f(g(h(x)))$, $\frac{dz}{dx} = f'(g(h(x))) \cdot g'(h(x)) \cdot h'(x)$ — each layer's local gradient is computed once and multiplied backward.

#### Q: Why is Adam not always the best choice despite being the most popular optimizer?
Adam maintains adaptive per-parameter learning rates using running estimates of the first moment (mean, $m_t$) and second moment (uncentered variance, $v_t$) of the gradients, which makes it fast to converge and robust to poorly-scaled gradients — a strong default. However, Adam's adaptive per-parameter scaling can lead to worse generalization than plain SGD with momentum in some settings (particularly large-scale image classification), because Adam converges to different, sometimes sharper minima that generalize less well, and it doesn't naturally implement the implicit regularization that SGD's noisier updates provide. In practice, many state-of-the-art vision models are still trained with SGD+momentum despite Adam's popularity for NLP/Transformer models, and AdamW (Adam with decoupled weight decay, fixing a subtle bug in how Adam originally combined L2 regularization with adaptive scaling) is now the more common recommendation than vanilla Adam.

#### Q: What's the difference between micro-F1 and macro-F1, and when does the choice matter most?
Macro-F1 computes F1 independently for each class and then takes the unweighted average — every class counts equally regardless of how many examples it has. Micro-F1 aggregates true positives, false positives, and false negatives globally across all classes before computing a single F1 — effectively weighting by class frequency, so it's dominated by performance on the majority class. The choice matters most under class imbalance: if you have 95% class A and 5% class B, micro-F1 will look good even if the model completely ignores class B, while macro-F1 will expose that failure since it weighs class B's poor F1 equally with class A's. Use macro-F1 when all classes matter equally regardless of frequency (e.g., rare disease detection across multiple disease types); use micro-F1 when overall example-level correctness is what matters.

#### Q: Why is PR-AUC preferred over ROC-AUC for imbalanced classification?
ROC-AUC plots True Positive Rate vs. False Positive Rate, both of which are computed relative to the size of their own respective class (TPR relative to all positives, FPR relative to all negatives). When negatives vastly outnumber positives, a model can produce a large absolute number of false positives while still keeping the false positive *rate* low (since the negative class is huge), making ROC-AUC look deceptively strong. Precision, by contrast, is directly sensitive to the absolute number of false positives relative to total positive predictions, so PR-AUC surfaces poor performance on the minority class that ROC-AUC can mask. Concrete example: fraud detection with 0.5% fraud rate — a model with a high ROC-AUC (0.95) can still have terrible precision at any reasonable recall threshold, which PR-AUC would reveal directly.

#### Q: When is R² misleading as a regression metric, and what would you check instead?
R² measures the proportion of variance in the target explained by the model relative to a naive mean-predicting baseline, but it can mislead in several ways: (1) it always increases (or stays the same) as you add more features, even irrelevant ones, making it a poor tool for comparing models with different numbers of features (use adjusted R² instead); (2) R² can be high even with systematic bias in a subset of the range (e.g., a model that fits the bulk of the data well but is badly wrong on a rare-but-important segment); (3) R² is not comparable across datasets with different target variances — a low R² on inherently noisy data doesn't mean the model is worse than a high-R² model on cleaner data. Always pair R² with residual plots (to check for non-random error patterns) and an absolute-error metric like MAE or RMSE that's interpretable in the target's original units.

#### Q: What's the difference between a parameter search using cross-validation and Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning?
Standard grid/random search evaluated via cross-validation treats each hyperparameter combination independently, wasting information from prior trials. Bayesian optimization builds a probabilistic surrogate model (commonly a Gaussian Process) of the objective function based on past evaluations, and uses it to choose the next point to evaluate by balancing exploration (uncertain regions) and exploitation (regions near known good results) — it typically outperforms random search substantially in the small-budget regime (few evaluations are expensive, e.g., training a large neural net), because it uses information from every prior trial intelligently, though its benefit shrinks as the budget grows large enough for random search to cover the space well anyway.

#### Q: Explain why L1 regularization produces sparse solutions but L2 does not.
L1 (Lasso) adds a penalty proportional to $\sum |w_i|$; its gradient with respect to each weight is a constant ($\pm\lambda$) regardless of the weight's magnitude, so it keeps "pushing" a weight toward exactly zero at a constant rate even as the weight gets small — once it reaches zero, the subgradient allows it to stay there since the L1 penalty is non-differentiable at zero, effectively making it a corner solution in weight-space. L2 (Ridge) adds a penalty proportional to $\sum w_i^2$; its gradient is proportional to the weight itself ($2\lambda w_i$), so as a weight shrinks toward zero, the pushing force shrinks too — it asymptotically approaches zero but never actually reaches it exactly. Geometrically, L1's constraint region is a diamond (with corners on the axes) while L2's is a circle/sphere — the sum-of-squared-error contours are more likely to intersect the L1 diamond at a corner (where one or more weights are exactly zero) than the smooth L2 sphere. Elastic Net combines both penalties to get some sparsity while retaining L2's stability with correlated features.

#### Q: Contrast BERT and GPT — architecture, pretraining objective, and typical use cases.
BERT is encoder-only and bidirectional: each token's representation attends to both left and right context simultaneously, and it's pretrained via Masked Language Modeling (predict randomly masked tokens using full surrounding context) plus (in the original paper) Next Sentence Prediction. This makes BERT well-suited for understanding tasks — classification, named entity recognition, extractive question answering — where you have the full input available and need a rich representation of it. GPT is decoder-only and causal: each token can only attend to previous tokens (via a triangular attention mask), and it's pretrained via next-token prediction (predict the next word given everything before it). This autoregressive setup is what makes GPT naturally suited to generation, since generating text requires producing one token at a time based only on what's been generated so far. Gotcha: fine-tuning a pretrained model means updating all (or an adapter subset of) its weights on task-specific data starting from pretrained weights — it is not training a transformer from scratch, which almost never works well on small task-specific datasets.

#### Q: Why do CNNs outperform plain MLPs on images, and what specific mechanism causes this?
The core mechanism is weight sharing combined with the local-connectivity structure of convolution. A fully-connected MLP layer on a 256×256 RGB image would need 196,608 independent weights per neuron in the first hidden layer just to look at the whole image once, with zero built-in assumption that nearby pixels are related. A 3×3 convolutional filter has only 27 parameters (3×3×3 channels) but is *reused* (slid) across every spatial location in the image — so the same edge-detector, say, is applied uniformly everywhere, which both slashes the parameter count by orders of magnitude and encodes the correct prior that "what a pattern is" matters more than "where it is" (translation invariance). Pooling then provides approximate local invariance to small spatial shifts. This parameter efficiency is precisely why CNNs were learnable from datasets of the size available in the early 2010s, whereas comparable MLPs would badly overfit or be computationally infeasible.

#### Q: What's the danger of applying the wrong data augmentation for a task?
Data augmentation directly encodes an invariance assumption into the model — if you augment with rotations, you're telling the model "orientation doesn't matter for this task's label." This is correct for tasks where the label genuinely is invariant to that transform (e.g., a skin lesion looks the same rotated, so rotation augmentation is appropriate and helpful) but actively harmful for tasks where orientation is meaningful (e.g., street sign classification, where a rotated "no entry" sign might resemble a different sign, or digit recognition, where a rotated "6" can look like a "9"). The model trained with harmful augmentation learns to be invariant to a transformation the real world requires it to be sensitive to, degrading real deployment accuracy even though training metrics may look fine. Always check whether the label is truly invariant to a proposed augmentation before applying it, and for detection/segmentation tasks remember that geometric augmentations (rotation, crop) must be applied consistently to the labels (bounding boxes, masks) as well as the image.

#### Q: Walk through the 5-step framework for approaching an ML system design interview question.
(1) **Problem Scoping** — clarify the objective, success metric, latency/throughput constraints, and how the ML metric maps to the actual business goal (e.g., CTR vs. long-term retention) before writing anything else; getting this wrong invalidates every downstream decision. (2) **Data Engineering** — define the feature and label sources, how they're joined, logging infrastructure, and how training-time computation will match serving-time computation. (3) **Modeling** — start with the simplest reasonable baseline (e.g., logistic regression or a heuristic) and justify each increment of complexity added afterward; don't jump straight to the most sophisticated architecture. (4) **Evaluation** — offline metrics (precision/recall/AUC as appropriate), then online validation via A/B testing or shadow mode before full rollout. (5) **Deployment** — serving infrastructure (batch vs. real-time), monitoring for drift, and a defined retraining cadence and rollback plan. Gotcha: candidates who skip step 1 and jump to model architecture make every subsequent choice arbitrary, since latency budget, labeling strategy, and cold-start handling are all determined by the problem scope.

#### Q: Why does the choice of offline (batch) vs. online (real-time) inference architecture matter so much, and how do you decide?
The latency requirement of the use case determines your entire feasible model space. Offline/batch inference (e.g., precomputing daily recommendations for all users) allows high throughput and low serving cost since predictions are computed once and cached/served from a lookup, but it can't react to real-time context (e.g., what a user just clicked seconds ago). Online/real-time inference (e.g., a fraud check on an in-flight transaction) requires the full prediction — feature computation plus model inference — to complete within a strict latency budget, often under 100ms, which constrains you toward smaller/quantized models, cascades, or precomputed embeddings with a lightweight scoring step at request time. Concrete example: serving personalized transformer-based ranking to 300M users at <200ms is infeasible as a single monolithic call — the fix is a two-stage architecture where cheap approximate nearest-neighbor retrieval narrows candidates first (offline-computable embeddings), and only a small final candidate set goes through the more expensive real-time ranking model.

#### Q: What is train-serve skew, and how do feature stores prevent it?
Train-serve skew occurs when the way a feature is computed during model training differs even slightly from how it's computed at serving time — different time windows for aggregation, different join logic, different null-handling — causing the model to silently receive different (and often subtly wrong) feature distributions in production than what it learned from during training. This is one of the hardest bugs to detect because there's no error thrown; the model simply degrades. Symptom pattern: strong offline validation metrics but poor and unexplained production performance. A feature store solves this by providing a single, shared feature computation layer used by both the training pipeline and the serving pipeline, so the exact same code (not just the same specification) computes a feature both when generating training data and when generating a live prediction request — removing the possibility of the two implementations drifting apart over time.

#### Q: How would you decide between a linear model and a deep neural network for a new tabular ML problem?
Always start with the simplest reasonable baseline — a linear or logistic regression, or a single gradient-boosted tree model — and only escalate to a DNN if: (1) you have a large enough dataset (rule of thumb >100K samples; DNNs are data-hungry and can badly overfit on small tabular datasets where tree ensembles like XGBoost typically win), (2) the problem genuinely has complex non-linear feature interactions that the baseline demonstrably fails to capture (verified by the baseline's validation performance plateauing well below business requirements), and (3) inference latency and interpretability requirements can tolerate the added complexity of a DNN (linear models remain far more interpretable, which matters for regulated domains needing auditable predictions). Interview gotcha: presenting a DNN as a default choice without first establishing that a simpler baseline is insufficient signals you skipped the "justify each increment of complexity" discipline that a senior engineer would apply.

#### Q: How would you design an LLM-powered feature for a product (e.g., a code review assistant)? What's the key tradeoff triangle?
The key tradeoff triangle is latency, cost, and quality — improving any two usually costs you on the third. Design steps: (1) define the acceptable latency budget for the feature's user experience (a synchronous inline suggestion needs far tighter latency than an asynchronous report); (2) choose model size accordingly — larger models give better quality but cost more and are slower, so match model size to the latency budget, potentially using a smaller distilled model for latency-critical paths and a larger model for async/batch paths; (3) cache repeated or similar queries aggressively since LLM calls are expensive and many real-world queries repeat or are near-duplicates; (4) implement a fallback (e.g., a simpler heuristic or cached prior response) for when the LLM call times out or fails; (5) build an evaluation pipeline before launch (a held-out set of representative queries with quality rubrics, since LLM outputs are open-ended and harder to evaluate than classification accuracy); (6) monitor for quality drift post-launch, since the LLM provider may update the underlying model or the input distribution may shift as usage patterns evolve.

#### Q: Explain how CLIP-style contrastive learning enables Google Photos to search images using arbitrary text queries it was never explicitly trained on.
CLIP is trained on large numbers of (image, text) pairs scraped from the web — image and its alt-text/caption — using a contrastive objective that pulls the embeddings of a true (image, caption) pair together in a shared embedding space while pushing mismatched pairs apart. This training signal ("this image and this caption co-occurred on a webpage") is weak and noisy compared to hand-curated labels, but at web scale the noise averages out, and the model learns a shared semantic space where images and their conceptual descriptions land near each other regardless of the exact category. This is why the model can "zero-shot" classify or retrieve images for text queries it was never explicitly trained on: it never learned "this is a dog" as a discrete label — it learned that image regions co-occurring with words like "dog" in captions across millions of examples occupy a particular region of the shared embedding space, so any new text query embeds into that same space and retrieves nearby images by similarity, with no need for a fixed, pre-defined label taxonomy.

#### Q: Use the STAR method to answer: "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a technical decision." What makes an answer here strong vs. weak?
A strong answer keeps Situation brief (under 30 seconds — just enough context to understand the stakes), states the Task in one sentence (your specific responsibility in that moment), and spends the majority of the answer on Action — using "I" consistently for what *you* personally did (the specific technical case you made, the data or analysis you brought, how you approached the disagreement collaboratively rather than adversarially) — before closing with a quantified Result. A weak answer either inflates the Situation into a long narrative that eats time meant for the substantive Action, uses "we" throughout in a way that makes it impossible for the interviewer to assess your individual contribution, or resolves the disagreement by simply asserting you were "right" without describing the reasoning or data that led to the outcome. The strongest version of this answer describes a case where you had partial information, made a specific, defensible decision using the information available, and can articulate the tradeoff you accepted — not a story where you were unambiguously correct and the other party was simply wrong.

#### Q: How do you defend a technical choice like "why did you use XGBoost over a linear model" in a way that demonstrates good engineering judgment?
A weak answer cites generic properties ("XGBoost is good for tabular data" or "it's popular and accurate"). A strong answer ties the choice explicitly to the constraints of that specific project: for example, "our 100K-row dataset had high feature sparsity and several categorical variables with non-linear interactions with the target; a linear model with L1 regularization would have required extensive manual feature engineering to capture those interactions, while XGBoost handles mixed feature types and interactions natively without that preprocessing burden, and its feature importance output satisfied our regulatory requirement for explainable decisions." The key structural elements interviewers look for: (1) name the alternatives you considered and specifically why you rejected each, not just why you picked the winner; (2) connect the choice to a concrete constraint (data size, latency, interpretability requirement, deployment timeline) rather than an abstract property of the algorithm; (3) be able to state one condition under which your choice would have been wrong, which demonstrates you understand the tradeoff rather than having a fixed favorite tool.

#### Q: What is the full LLM training pipeline at a high level: pretraining, SFT, and RLHF/DPO?
**Pretraining**: next-token prediction on massive unlabeled text corpora — the model learns a broad, compressed model of language statistics and world knowledge with no human labels required; this is the most compute-intensive and foundational stage. **Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)**: the pretrained model is further trained on curated (prompt, ideal response) pairs to teach it the desired output format, tone, and instruction-following behavior — relatively cheap, but bounded by the quality and coverage of the annotated examples. **RLHF/DPO**: a further alignment stage that optimizes the model directly toward human preferences (via a reward model + RL, or directly via preference pairs), pushing quality beyond what SFT's imitation-only signal achieves.

#### Q: When would you choose RAG over fine-tuning for adapting an LLM to a specific knowledge domain, and vice versa?
Choose RAG when: the underlying knowledge is dynamic and changes frequently (fine-tuning would require constant retraining to stay current), you need to provide citations/traceability back to source documents (a legal or medical use case where "here's where this came from" matters), or you cannot afford the GPU compute and engineering overhead of a fine-tuning pipeline. Choose fine-tuning when: the task requires adapting the model's output format, tone, or style rather than injecting new factual knowledge (RAG doesn't help with "sound more like our brand voice"), the underlying knowledge is largely stable and won't need frequent updates, low-latency inference is critical and you can't afford the extra retrieval hop's latency cost, or you've found that RAG's retrieval quality is consistently and irreducibly poor for your domain (e.g., highly specialized jargon that embedding models handle badly). In practice, the two are often combined: fine-tune for format/behavior, then use RAG on top for up-to-date factual grounding.

#### Q: What's the difference between few-shot prompting and chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting, and when does each help most?
Few-shot prompting includes several (typically 3-8) complete input/output examples directly in the prompt, relying on the model's in-context learning ability to infer the desired task format and behavior from the pattern of examples — this is most effective for structured output tasks where the exact format matters (e.g., extracting fields into a specific JSON schema) and there's no room for the model to guess wrong on formatting. Chain-of-thought prompting instead asks the model to reason step-by-step before producing its final answer (e.g., appending "Let's think step by step," or showing worked-out reasoning traces as the few-shot examples), which significantly improves performance on tasks requiring multi-step logical or arithmetic reasoning, because it gives the model's forward-pass "more computation" to work with before committing to a final answer — the model isn't forced to produce the correct answer in a single token-generation step, but can build up intermediate conclusions. Gotcha: CoT tends to help most on reasoning-heavy tasks (math word problems, multi-hop QA) and can add unnecessary verbosity/cost with little benefit on tasks that are already simple lookups or classifications where reasoning steps don't change the answer.

#### Q: How would you handle a system that needs real-time predictions but your best model is too slow to meet the latency budget?
Several complementary approaches, roughly in order of effort: (1) model compression — quantization (reduce weight precision, e.g., float32 → int8) or distillation (train a smaller "student" model to mimic the large "teacher" model's outputs) to shrink inference cost directly; (2) result caching — if the same or similar inputs recur, cache predictions and serve from cache instead of recomputing (works well when the input space is not fully unique per request, e.g., popular items/queries); (3) a two-stage cascade — a cheap, fast model handles the bulk of easy cases within budget, escalating only the uncertain/hard cases to the expensive model, so average latency stays low even though the expensive model alone would violate the budget; (4) asynchronous pre-computation — if some part of the prediction can be computed ahead of the request (e.g., overnight batch scoring of likely candidates), only a cheap final lookup or light re-ranking needs to happen in the real-time path. The right combination depends on whether the bottleneck is compute cost per request, request volume, or the fraction of genuinely novel/uncachable inputs.

#### Q: What structural elements should every technical answer include in an interview, regardless of topic?
A strong technical answer follows: (1) a direct statement of your position or answer up front — don't bury the conclusion at the end of a long explanation, since interviewers are also assessing communication clarity, and a rambling build-up before the point makes an answer hard to follow under time pressure; (2) intuition or an analogy that makes the mechanism accessible, calibrated to the audience (a product manager needs a different analogy for gradient descent than a fellow ML engineer does); (3) an explicit production tradeoff or caveat — naming what you gave up or what would break your answer's assumptions ("I chose simplicity over accuracy because the deployment timeline was fixed" is more credible than implying you found a solution with no downsides, since real engineering always involves tradeoffs and pretending otherwise reads as either inexperience or dishonesty). Combined with sketching diagrams for system design questions (labeling data flow and axes) and keeping delivery under about 90 seconds for purely factual questions, this structure demonstrates the same rigor in communication that the interview is trying to assess in your technical reasoning.

#### Q: What are the highest-value things to verify the night before an ML interview, given that no new learning should happen at that point?
Per the final-prep readiness checklist: confirm you can explain your two most important projects in under three minutes each (a rambling project walkthrough burns interview time that should go to deeper technical follow-ups); have three thoughtful, specific questions ready for the interviewer (generic questions like "what's a typical day like" are weaker signal than "how does the team handle model versioning" or "what does on-call look like for production models," which demonstrate you're already thinking about the production dimension of the role); make sure you've done at least one full timed mock interview spoken aloud, not rehearsed silently in your head, since verbal fluency under time pressure is a distinct skill from silent comprehension; and confirm logistics (webcam, microphone, internet, and dev environment) are working well in advance, since a last-minute technical failure is an entirely avoidable and unnecessary source of stress. The guiding principle for week 4 overall is that the interview tests retrieval and application fluency of material you already know, not the addition of new material — cramming a new topic the night before typically produces shallow, unreliable recall that performs worse under pressure than confidently applying a smaller set of well-internalized fundamentals.

#### Q: What is the reward hacking problem in RLHF, and how would you mitigate it?
RLHF is susceptible to reward hacking: because the policy is optimized against a learned reward model rather than true human judgment directly, the policy can learn to exploit quirks or blind spots in the reward model to score highly without genuinely improving the response quality a human would perceive — for example, generating overly long or superficially confident-sounding responses if the reward model has learned to associate those surface features with quality. Mitigations include careful reward model design and data diversity, a KL penalty against the SFT policy to prevent the policy from drifting too far in reward-hacking directions, and ongoing evaluation against real human judgment rather than trusting the reward model's score in isolation.

## Hard

#### Q: Explain the $\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling factor in the self-attention formula.
The formula is $\text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$. Without scaling, the dot products $QK^T$ grow in magnitude proportionally to $d_k$ (the dimensionality of the query/key vectors), since each dot product sums $d_k$ terms with roughly zero mean — larger $d_k$ means larger variance in the raw dot product values. Very large or very small logits pushed into softmax cause it to saturate (producing near one-hot outputs), which in turn produces extremely small gradients during backpropagation (the softmax's gradient is proportional to $p(1-p)$, which vanishes near 0 or 1) — essentially a vanishing gradient problem localized inside the attention mechanism. Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ keeps the dot product variance roughly constant regardless of dimensionality, keeping the softmax in a well-behaved gradient regime.

#### Q: What is the vanishing gradient problem, and how do modern architectures address it?
In deep networks trained with backprop, the gradient at an early layer is the product of many local derivatives from all the later layers (chain rule). If those local derivatives are consistently less than 1 in magnitude (as with sigmoid/tanh activations, whose derivative maxes out at 0.25 and 0.25 respectively, and near-zero away from the origin due to saturation), the product shrinks exponentially with depth, so early layers receive a vanishingly small training signal and barely update. Fixes: (1) ReLU and its variants (Leaky ReLU, GELU) have derivative exactly 1 in the active region, avoiding multiplicative shrinkage; (2) residual/skip connections (ResNet-style) let gradients flow directly through an identity path, bypassing the multiplicative chain; (3) careful weight initialization (Xavier/He) keeps activations in a well-scaled range from the start; (4) batch normalization keeps layer inputs in a stable distribution throughout training, reducing the chance of activations drifting into saturating regions.

#### Q: Explain the reparameterization trick used in VAEs. Why is it necessary?
A Variational Autoencoder needs to sample a latent variable $z$ from a distribution parameterized by the encoder's output ($\mu$, $\sigma$), but sampling is a stochastic, non-differentiable operation — you cannot backpropagate a gradient through a random sampling step directly. The reparameterization trick rewrites the sample as $z = \mu + \sigma \cdot \epsilon$, where $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ is drawn independently of the network's parameters. This moves all the randomness into $\epsilon$, an external input, so $z$ becomes a deterministic, differentiable function of $\mu$ and $\sigma$ — gradients can now flow from the reconstruction loss back through $z$ into the encoder's parameters via standard backprop.

#### Q: Explain why nested cross-validation is necessary when you're doing hyperparameter tuning.
If you tune hyperparameters using cross-validation and then report the best CV score you found as your model's expected performance, you're overfitting to the validation folds — the hyperparameter search itself has "seen" and adapted to those folds, so the reported score has optimism bias built in. Nested CV separates this: an inner loop performs hyperparameter search using cross-validation on a subset of the data (e.g., via Optuna or grid search), and an outer loop holds out a completely separate fold for final, unbiased evaluation of the whole tuning process. The gap between the inner loop's best reported CV score and the outer loop's held-out score is a direct measurement of your search's optimism bias. Gotcha: standard (non-nested) CV for tuning is fine for *choosing* hyperparameters, but the number it reports should never be quoted as the model's expected generalization performance.

#### Q: You get 5-fold CV scores of [0.95, 0.60, 0.91, 0.89, 0.92]. What's your first action before averaging?
Do not average blindly — investigate the outlier fold (0.60) before treating it as noise. First check whether that fold has a data quality issue: is there a rare class concentrated in that fold (suggesting you should use stratified K-fold instead of plain K-fold), a temporal artifact (if the data has any time structure and folds weren't shuffled properly), or a bug in preprocessing that only manifests for that specific fold's split (e.g., a category present in test but not seen in train, causing an encoding failure). Only after ruling out a pipeline bug or systematic issue should you treat the variance as real model instability and report the mean ± standard deviation rather than a single average, since a single low fold indicates the model's performance may not be reliable across all data segments.

#### Q: Why does Vision Transformer (ViT) need much more training data than a CNN to reach comparable performance?
ViT treats an image as a sequence of flattened patches with no built-in locality or translation-invariance assumptions (unlike a CNN's convolutional structure) — it must *learn* these useful spatial priors purely from data via self-attention, rather than having them baked into the architecture. CNN inductive biases act as an implicit regularizer: they constrain the hypothesis space to functions that respect locality and translation-invariance, which is usually correct for natural images and lets a CNN generalize well from relatively little data. Without that built-in constraint, ViT has a much larger effective hypothesis space and needs proportionally more data to find good weights that don't overfit — empirically, ViT underperforms ResNet-style CNNs on ImageNet-scale data (~1M images) but surpasses them when pretrained on datasets like JFT-300M (~300M images), where the abundance of data lets the model learn the useful spatial structure on its own, potentially discovering patterns a CNN's rigid locality assumption would have prevented it from ever representing.

#### Q: Design a cascading classifier system for fraud detection under a 50ms latency budget. Why does the first stage's recall matter more than its precision?
Architecture: Rule engine (~0ms, catches obvious fraud patterns) → cheap model like Logistic Regression or a small XGBoost (~10ms, filters the bulk of easy true-negatives) → expensive model like a DNN sequence model (~30ms, only run on the minority of transactions that pass stage 1) → combined score determines block/review/allow. The stages are not independent — whatever the first stage filters out never reaches the second stage at all, so if the cheap model has low recall (misses true fraud cases by scoring them low), those fraud cases are permanently lost regardless of how good the expensive downstream model is. Precision at stage 1 matters less because false positives (legitimate transactions incorrectly passed to stage 2) just cost a bit of extra compute at stage 2 — a recoverable cost — whereas false negatives at stage 1 (fraud incorrectly filtered out) are unrecoverable, since stage 2 never gets a chance to catch them. This is why you tune the cheap model's threshold to prioritize high recall (e.g., recall=0.95, precision=0.3 is acceptable) even at the cost of stage 2 processing more borderline cases.

#### Q: Why did the YouTube recommendation system's paper use watch time instead of click-through rate (CTR) as the training objective, and what's the deeper lesson?
A model trained to maximize CTR learns to recommend content that is clicked frequently — which is not the same as content users actually find valuable, and this specifically incentivizes clickbait: titles/thumbnails engineered to generate a click that is quickly abandoned. Watch time forces the model to predict a behavior (sustained engagement) that correlates much more closely with genuine user value, because a user who abandons a video immediately after clicking provides a clear negative signal that CTR alone would miss. The deeper lesson for system design interviews: the metric you choose to optimize is not a neutral technical detail — it directly shapes what behavior the model learns to produce, and there is almost always a gap between what's easy to measure (clicks) and what you actually want to maximize (long-term user satisfaction/retention). A model that perfectly optimizes even a well-chosen metric like watch time can still recommend addictive-but-regretted content, which is why production systems add auxiliary constraints and re-ranking rules beyond the single primary objective.

#### Q: Why does Google Search use a modular architecture (separate models for spelling, synonyms, semantic intent, and ranking) instead of one end-to-end model?
This is a system-level bias-variance tradeoff. Each specialized module has lower variance/error on its narrow sub-task than a single monolithic model trying to jointly handle every aspect of query understanding would, and — more importantly for production — modularity allows independent iteration: the semantic understanding module (BERT-based) can be improved, retrained, or replaced without risking a regression in the spelling correction module, and a bug in one module doesn't take down the whole pipeline. A single end-to-end model could in principle optimize globally (an advantage true end-to-end systems have), but it becomes much harder to debug (a wrong result gives no visibility into which sub-capability failed), harder to update incrementally (any retrain risks regressing unrelated capabilities), and any single failure mode affects the entire system's output simultaneously. Modularity explicitly trades some global optimization potential for debuggability, reliability, and independent-team velocity — a tradeoff that matters enormously at Google's scale and iteration cadence.

#### Q: How would you think about fairness in a recommendation system where the top 1% of creators receive 10x the exposure of the remaining 99% combined?
This cannot be answered as simply "yes it's unfair" or "no it's fine" — it requires first defining which fairness criterion you're checking against, since several reasonable and mutually conflicting definitions exist: "equal exposure" for all creators regardless of quality, "exposure proportional to quality/engagement" (which could still produce very skewed outcomes if quality itself is skewed), or "equal exposure conditional on demonstrated user interest." You'd need to measure whether the skew reflects genuine, broad-based quality/preference signal, or whether it's a feedback loop artifact — content that received early exposure (perhaps due to initial randomness or a launch-time promotion) accumulated more engagement data, which then caused the model to keep recommending it more, compounding the disparity regardless of relative quality (a classic rich-get-richer dynamic in recommender systems). Also critical: removing "creator identity" or other sensitive attributes from the feature set does not remove bias, since the model can proxy those attributes through correlated features (e.g., posting time, content category, follower count as a proxy for identity-correlated factors) — fairness interventions need to happen at the data collection, labeling, and objective-definition stages, not as a post-hoc feature-removal step.

#### Q: A model's performance drops suddenly in production. Walk through your diagnostic process, and explain why "suddenly" is an important clue.
A *sudden* drop (today vs. yesterday) is a strong signal pointing toward an infrastructure or pipeline issue rather than the model itself — model/concept drift is typically gradual, developing over days or weeks as the underlying data distribution shifts, so a cliff-edge drop suggests something changed abruptly, like a code deployment, a schema change in an upstream data source, or a broken ETL job. Diagnostic order: (1) check what was deployed or changed in the last 24 hours across the whole stack, not just the model — a feature pipeline change, a dependency upgrade, or an API contract change upstream are common culprits; (2) if nothing was deployed, compare current input feature distributions against a training/baseline distribution feature-by-feature to check for a sudden data pipeline break (e.g., a field starts arriving null, or with a unit/format that changed silently); (3) if feature statistics look stable, check whether the label distribution or label collection process changed (e.g., a labeling backlog introducing lag, or a business rule change altering what counts as the positive class); (4) only after ruling out infrastructure and labeling issues would you investigate genuine gradual concept drift as the cause, and note that concept drift by definition wouldn't explain a sudden, discontinuous drop.

#### Q: Explain the full LLM alignment pipeline's RLHF vs. DPO tradeoff in depth — what does each optimize, and what flexibility does DPO give up?
**RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback)**: a separate reward model is trained on human preference comparisons (given two candidate responses, which is better?), and then PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) is used to fine-tune the LLM's policy to maximize the reward model's score — more expensive and complex than SFT but can push quality beyond what SFT alone achieves, since it optimizes directly for what humans actually prefer rather than imitating a fixed set of curated examples. **DPO (Direct Preference Optimization)**: bypasses training a separate reward model entirely, directly optimizing the LLM on preference data using a closed-form loss derived to be mathematically equivalent to the RLHF objective under certain assumptions — simpler to implement and more stable to train than PPO, and increasingly preferred in practice, though it loses the flexibility of being able to adjust the reward function independently of the policy (with RLHF, you can swap in a different reward model without retraining from scratch; with DPO, changing the objective typically requires retraining from the SFT checkpoint). The deeper tradeoff: RLHF's explicit reward model is a reusable, inspectable artifact that decouples "what we reward" from "how we optimize," while DPO fuses those into a single training step, trading modularity for simplicity and training stability.

#### Q: What is paged attention (as in vLLM), and what specific memory problem does it solve beyond the basic KV cache?
The KV cache reduces the per-step cost of autoregressive generation, but it creates a new problem: KV caches have variable length per request (different prompts/generations have different lengths), and naive implementations allocate cache memory in large contiguous blocks sized for the worst case, causing severe GPU memory fragmentation and wasted capacity when serving many concurrent requests of different lengths. Paged Attention (used in vLLM) borrows the idea of OS virtual memory paging: it manages the KV cache in fixed-size, non-contiguous memory "pages" that can be allocated and freed flexibly per request, dramatically reducing fragmentation and letting a GPU serve many more concurrent requests from the same memory budget. This is what allows high-throughput multi-tenant LLM serving without over-provisioning GPU memory for worst-case sequence lengths on every request.

#### Q: Describe the RAG pipeline architecture end-to-end, and name a failure mode at each stage.
Pipeline: Query → (optional) Query Rewriting/expansion via an LLM → Embedding the query → Vector DB approximate nearest-neighbor search (e.g., FAISS, Pinecone) to retrieve top-k candidate chunks → (optional) Cross-encoder reranker to refine the top-k down to a smaller top-n → LLM generates the final answer conditioned on the retrieved context → citations extracted and attached from the source chunks used. Failure modes at each stage: **Embedding/chunking** — chunks too long dilute relevant signal, chunks too short lose necessary context, or using an embedding model poorly suited to the domain's vocabulary (e.g., generic embeddings on highly technical/legal text). **Retrieval** — low recall, where the actually-relevant chunk simply isn't retrieved because it's semantically distant from the literal query wording (motivating hybrid sparse+dense retrieval or query rewriting), or high latency from an unoptimized/non-approximate index. **Reranking** — a cross-encoder reranker adds meaningful latency (100-200ms), so it's often a bottleneck to skip for tight p50 latency SLAs but valuable to enable when p99 quality matters more than raw speed. **Generation** — hallucination when the retrieved context is insufficient or contradictory, or the model ignoring the provided context entirely and falling back on its parametric (pretrained) knowledge, producing an answer inconsistent with the retrieved sources.

#### Q: In a mock interview, you're asked about an algorithm or paper you've never heard of. What's the right way to respond?
The wrong responses are: staying silent or saying "I don't know" and stopping there (this gives the interviewer zero signal about your reasoning ability), or confidently fabricating an incorrect explanation (this is actively worse, since it can be probed and exposed, and it damages trust in everything else you've said). The right response is to reason explicitly from first principles and from the constraints of the problem the technique likely addresses: state what class of problem this seems to be solving based on its name/context, name the properties you'd expect a solution to that problem to need (e.g., "if this is for sequential data with long-range dependencies, I'd expect it to address vanishing gradients and support variable-length inputs — that points toward some attention-based mechanism"), and reason toward a plausible structure while being explicit that you're reconstructing rather than recalling. Interviewers frequently ask about things deliberately outside a candidate's known experience specifically to observe this reasoning process — demonstrating principled first-principles thinking under uncertainty is often more valuable signal to them than a memorized correct answer would have been.

#### Q: What's your systematic process for diagnosing why a model has 0.92 AUC in staging but 0.81 AUC in production?
This staging-to-production gap is one of the most common real ML production problems, and a systematic answer rules out causes in order rather than guessing: (1) **train-serve skew in feature computation** — check whether staging and production compute the same features using genuinely identical code paths (not just the same specification), since subtle differences in time windows, joins, or null-handling are the most common silent cause; (2) **data distribution shift between staging and production** — staging data is often a curated or historical snapshot that doesn't reflect the true live traffic distribution (different user segments, seasonality, or an adversarial population like fraud that specifically doesn't appear in staging); (3) **label leakage in the training pipeline that isn't present at inference** — a feature available at training time (perhaps computed with a slight lookahead) that isn't actually available in real-time at prediction time, inflating offline metrics artificially; (4) **temporal feature behavior differences** — staging often uses static historical data for time-dependent features, while production computes them in real-time, which can introduce timing lag, staleness, or race conditions that staging never exercised. A rigorous answer would state you'd check each of these in turn with concrete diagnostics (e.g., logging training-time feature values and comparing their distribution against production-served feature values for the same entities) rather than jumping to "retrain the model," which doesn't address any of these root causes if one of them is present.

#### Q: Derive why softmax combined with cross-entropy loss produces such a clean gradient, and why this matters for training stability.
For a multi-class classifier with softmax output $p_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}}$ and cross-entropy loss $L = -\sum_i y_i \log p_i$ against one-hot target $y$, the gradient with respect to the pre-softmax logit $z_k$ simplifies to $\frac{\partial L}{\partial z_k} = p_k - y_k$ — the predicted probability minus the true label, regardless of how many classes there are or how the intermediate softmax terms look. This is a non-obvious cancellation: naively, you'd expect the chain rule through softmax's normalization term (a sum over all classes) to produce a messy expression, but the log in cross-entropy exactly cancels softmax's exponential, leaving a linear, well-scaled error signal. This matters for training stability because the gradient magnitude is bounded in $[-1, 1]$ per class and is directly proportional to how wrong the prediction is, avoiding both vanishing gradients (from softmax saturation, which would occur if you used a different loss like MSE on top of softmax probabilities) and exploding gradients — it's the same underlying mechanism as the sigmoid+binary-cross-entropy gradient in binary classification, generalized to multiple classes.

#### Q: Why can a model with excellent calibration still have poor discriminative power (and vice versa), and how would you diagnose which problem you have?
Calibration measures whether predicted probabilities match observed frequencies (e.g., among all cases where the model predicts 70% probability of positive, roughly 70% should actually be positive) — it's an average, property computed over bins of predicted probability, and says nothing about whether the model can separate individual positive and negative examples' scores. Discrimination (measured by AUC) measures whether positive examples tend to receive higher scores than negative examples, regardless of whether the absolute score values mean anything as probabilities. A model that always predicts the base rate (e.g., 5% for every example, if 5% is the true positive rate) is perfectly calibrated on average but has zero discriminative power (AUC = 0.5) since it can't rank any example above another. Conversely, a model can have excellent AUC (it correctly ranks positives above negatives) while being badly miscalibrated (e.g., all its probability outputs are systematically too high or too low, or its outputs are correct in relative order but the raw scores from something like an SVM margin or an unbalanced-class model don't reflect true probabilities at all) — rescaling/calibrating (Platt scaling, isotonic regression) can fix this without touching the ranking. Diagnose with a reliability diagram (calibration curve) for calibration and ROC-AUC/precision-recall curves for discrimination; the two failure modes require entirely different fixes — calibration issues are fixed post-hoc with a rescaling step, discrimination issues require better features or a better model, since no amount of rescaling can create ranking ability the underlying model doesn't have.
